Quick Summary

The Monty Hall problem is a probability puzzle named after the original host of "Let's Make a Deal", Monty Hall.

It presents a game-show-like scenario where the "contestant" is presented with three closed doors. Behind two of the doors is a goat, and behind the other door there is a car. Obviously, the contestant wants the car. The contestant picks one door, but does not open it.

There are then two doors remaining, at least one of which must contain a goat. The host opens an unpicked door that contains a goat and asks the contestant if they would like to change their door to the last remaining door.

Here is where the "probability" part of the puzzle comes in. If the contestant chooses to change their door at this point, they have a 2 in 3 chance of getting the car, but if they do not change their door, they have only a 1 in 3 chance.

This can seem counterintuitive at first, because it seems as though since only two doors remain, one which definitely contains a goat and one which definitely contains a car, there should be a 50-50 chance that your door contains the car.

This web application was made to let you see for certain that the odds are in fact 2 in 3 if you change your door.

Go ahead and pick one of the doors above, and after playing through 50 times or so (alternating whether or not you change your door occasionally) you should see in the stats table above that the ratio of losses to wins when you choose to change your door is about 33.33% (i.e. you lose one third of the time), and that the ratio of losses to wins when you choose to keep your door is about 66.66% (i.e. you lose two thirds of the time).
